Draw-Level Vintage Curves

Executive Summary

How we attribute delinquent balances to individual draws for vintage performance analysis

The Business Problem

Each Line of Credit (LOC) account can have multiple draws—separate disbursements of money made at different times. When an account becomes delinquent or is charged off, we know the total delinquent balance, but not how much of that balance belongs to each individual draw.

Why does this matter? To build accurate vintage curves (how money funded in Month X performs over time), we need to know what portion of delinquency is attributable to each draw, based on when it was funded. Without this, we cannot assess whether fundings made in a particular month or quarter are performing better or worse than others.

Example: One Account, Three Draws

January 2024 — Draw A: $10,000
First disbursement. Account begins making payments.
April 2024 — Draw B: $10,000
Second disbursement. By now, 15% of the original balance has been paid down.
October 2024 — Draw C: $10,000
Third disbursement. By now, 40% of the original balance has been paid down.
July 2025 — Account charges off with $18,000 balance
How much of this $18,000 belongs to each draw?

A naive approach would split the $18,000 proportionally by funded amount: $6,000 each. But this ignores timing—Draw A has been paying down for 18 months while Draw C only for 9 months. More of Draw C's original funding should still be outstanding.

How the Model Works

The model uses a payment-based decay approach to estimate how much of each draw has been paid down over time, then distributes the actual account balance proportionally.

1 Identify Draws Find every disbursement for each LOC account with its date and amount

2 Track Account Paydown Observe what fraction of the account balance gets paid off each month
→
3 Attribute to Draws Estimate each draw's remaining balance based on how much paydown occurred since that draw was funded
→
4 Normalize & Report Scale the estimates so they sum exactly to the actual account balance, then split into delinquency buckets

The Key Insight: Newer Draws Carry More Weight

When a draw is made early in the account's life, it has been subject to many months of payments. When a draw is made later, it arrives after significant paydown has already occurred, so proportionally more of it is still outstanding. The model captures this by giving newer draws a higher weight.

Share Stability Over Time

Once all draws have been made, each draw's share of the account balance is permanently fixed. The shares are determined at the moment each draw is funded and do not change afterward—regardless of whether the account is current, delinquent, or charged off.

The only event that changes shares is a new draw being funded, which is a real business event that legitimately changes the composition of the account.

While the absolute dollar amounts attributed to each draw may change if the account's total balance is adjusted (e.g., due to recoveries), the percentage share of each draw remains locked in.

What this guarantees
  • No draw ever disappears from the model—every draw is tracked for the lifetime of the account
  • Vintage curves reflect the true performance of each funding cohort without distortion
  • Charged-off accounts maintain accurate, stable attribution indefinitely

How to Read a Vintage Curve Built from This Data

Pick a funding cohort (e.g., "Q1-2024 fundings"). For each month since funding (1, 2, 3, …), sum the attributed delinquent balances across all draws in that cohort and divide by the total funded amount. The resulting curve shows what percentage of Q1-2024 fundings are delinquent at each point in time—the classic vintage curve, but now at the draw level.

One-Page Summary

QuestionAnswer
What does the model do? Takes account-level delinquent balances and splits them across individual draws, weighted by how long each draw has been paying down
Why is this needed? Vintage performance analysis requires knowing how fundings from a specific period are performing. Since the bank tracks balances at the account level, we need a model to allocate down to the draw level.
Why not split evenly by funded amount? Newer draws have had less time to be paid down and therefore represent a larger share of the remaining balance. An even split would undercount newer draws and overcount older ones.
Are the shares stable? Yes. Once all draws are funded, each draw's share is permanently fixed. Shares only change when a new draw is made—a real business event.
What about charged-off accounts? Shares remain stable indefinitely. Every draw is tracked for the lifetime of the account with no cutoff horizon. Absolute dollar amounts may shift if recoveries change the total balance, but each draw's percentage share stays constant.
How is this used in Tableau? The output feeds directly into Tableau dashboards for vintage curve analysis. Data can be sliced by funding quarter, risk rating, partner, channel, product type, FICO, and other business dimensions.
